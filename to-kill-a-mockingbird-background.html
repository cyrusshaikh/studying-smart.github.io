To Kill a Mockingbird

by Harper Lee

Background

Historical and Social Backdrop

To Kill a Mockingbird takes place during a turbulent period in American history, covering the years 1933 to 1935—years deeply marked by both the Great Depression and the lingering oppression of Jim Crow laws in the post-Civil War South. Although Harper Lee sets the story in the fictional town of Maycomb, Alabama, the town closely mirrors the realities of countless small Southern communities during that era. Maycomb’s landscape is shaped by both financial hardship and rigid social divisions, with family reputation, race, and class playing central roles in determining one’s place in the community.

The devastating impact of the Great Depression looms throughout the novel. Economic despair touches nearly every family, though to varying degrees. The Cunninghams and Ewells, for instance, represent the crushing poverty of rural, working-class white families, while the town’s professional class, including characters like Atticus Finch, manage to live with relative stability, though not untouched by financial strain. Lee captures these economic realities not only through setting and plot, but through the way characters speak and behave—such as when Atticus, after receiving gifts of food from Black townspeople, remarks simply that “times are too hard.”

Layered atop this financial struggle is the deeply entrenched racial injustice enforced by Jim Crow laws, which governed much of Southern society both during the novel’s setting in the 1930s and at the time of its publication in 1960. Although thirty years separate the historical period depicted from the era when Lee wrote the novel, little had changed regarding the legal and social marginalization of African Americans. The segregated courthouse, the rigid color line dividing the church congregations, and the all-white jury that condemns Tom Robinson are all reflections of systemic racism that governed every level of Southern life.

Lee’s portrayal of Maycomb extends beyond the legal system, exposing the quiet hypocrisy that allowed racist ideologies to flourish among seemingly good and respectable people. Through the innocent but observant perspective of Scout, Lee reveals contradictions that children struggle to comprehend. For instance, Scout is bewildered when her teacher, Miss Gates, passionately condemns Hitler's persecution of the Jews while simultaneously expressing prejudice toward Black neighbors in her own community. This disconnect illustrates how deeply racism was woven into everyday moral blindness, even among those who believed themselves to be virtuous.

In this way, Lee's novel offers more than a simple critique of individual prejudice—it confronts the entire structure of social injustice, arguing for the fundamental equality and dignity of all people, regardless of race, class, or creed. While deeply rooted in its historical setting, To Kill a Mockingbird ultimately voices concerns that continue to resonate across generations.

Harper Lee and the Origins of To Kill a Mockingbird

Though Harper Lee was famously private and rarely granted interviews or public appearances, her personal history offers rich insights into the origins of To Kill a Mockingbird. Much of the novel’s world draws directly from her own childhood experiences growing up in Monroeville, Alabama—a small town that bears clear resemblance to Maycomb. Even the film adaptation of To Kill a Mockingbird filmed its courtroom scenes in Monroeville, further linking the fictional setting to Lee’s real-life hometown.

Born in 1926, Lee was the same age as Scout Finch during the pivotal events of the novel, and like Scout, she grew up observing the legal profession closely. Her father, Amasa Coleman Lee, served both as a lawyer and as a member of the Alabama State Legislature during Lee’s youth, holding office between 1926 and 1938. Much like Atticus Finch, Lee's father once defended two Black men accused of murder—a direct parallel to the fictional trial of Tom Robinson that stands at the heart of the novel.

The national context of Lee’s early years also left its imprint on her storytelling. In 1931, when Lee was just six years old, the infamous Scottsboro Boys case unfolded. Nine Black teenagers were accused of raping two white women—a case that epitomized the racial injustices embedded in the American legal system. While Lee never explicitly cites the Scottsboro trial as a source for her novel, its similarities to Tom Robinson’s fate suggest that this highly publicized event shaped her awareness of Southern racism and legal inequity.

One of the novel’s most beloved characters, Dill, was inspired by Lee’s real-life childhood friend Truman Capote, who visited Monroeville each summer. Like Dill, Capote was imaginative, curious, and deeply close to Lee as a child. Capote would later gain his own literary fame with works such as Breakfast at Tiffany’s and In Cold Blood, the latter of which Harper Lee helped research and prepare.

For most of her life, To Kill a Mockingbird remained Lee’s sole published work. However, in 2015, one year before her death, another novel titled Go Set a Watchman was released under her name. The publication was surrounded by significant debate and controversy, as it was ultimately revealed to be an early draft of To Kill a Mockingbird rather than a true sequel. In Go Set a Watchman, Scout returns to Maycomb as an adult and confronts the complexity of her father’s moral views within a changing social landscape. Many have questioned whether Lee fully consented to the manuscript's publication, raising ethical concerns given her advanced age and declining health at the time.

Regardless of the controversy surrounding Go Set a Watchman, Harper Lee’s To Kill a Mockingbird remains her most influential and celebrated literary contribution. Through its vivid characters, moral clarity, and fearless examination of social injustice, Lee crafted a timeless work that continues to challenge and inspire readers around the world.
