To Kill a Mockingbird

by Harper Lee

Chapter Summaries: Chapters 21-24

Chapter 21 Calpurnia delivers a note from Aunt Alexandra, concerned that the children have been missing. The courtroom soon learns that Scout, Jem, and Dill have been watching the trial from the balcony reserved for the Black community. Though Atticus is initially upset, he allows them to return after supper to hear the verdict. Reverend Sykes saves seats for them in the balcony. The jury deliberates for hours, indicating some internal conflict. Eventually, they return with a guilty verdict, confirming that Tom Robinson has been convicted despite overwhelming evidence in his favor. As the courtroom empties, the Black spectators in the balcony stand silently as Atticus walks beneath them—a gesture of deep respect and gratitude. Chapter 21 Analysis The drawn-out jury deliberation offers a small glimmer of hope that at least one juror considered Tom's innocence. Yet, the guilty verdict ultimately reflects the town’s entrenched racism. The act of the Black community standing as Atticus exits the courtroom is one of the novel’s most powerful nonverbal moments. While the white community condemns him for defending Tom, the Black citizens honor him for his moral courage. This moment highlights the contrast between legal failure and moral victory. Chapter 22 Jem is devastated by the verdict, weeping not only for Tom but for the injustice itself. Atticus explains with resignation that such outcomes are common, though children like Jem still possess the capacity to weep at injustice—a purity adults have often lost. The next morning, the Black community delivers food to the Finch household in gratitude for Atticus’s efforts. Atticus is deeply moved by this gesture of respect, even as he continues to bear the weight of the injustice. Miss Maudie attempts to console Jem and Scout, explaining that there are people like Atticus who take on difficult moral tasks when others refuse. Their conversation is interrupted when Miss Stephanie reports that Bob Ewell spat in Atticus's face, threatening him for shaming the Ewells during the trial. Chapter 22 Analysis This chapter shifts from legal tragedy to moral reflection. Jem’s tears signify his loss of childhood innocence. Atticus, while wounded by the verdict, models the same stoic integrity he has displayed throughout. The African American community’s silent gratitude contrasts sharply with Bob Ewell’s public resentment, emphasizing the toxic pride and fragility that underpin Maycomb’s racist structures. Maudie’s remark about "men who are born to do our unpleasant jobs" casts Atticus as a Christlike figure—sacrificing his comfort and safety for the sake of justice. Chapter 23 Bob Ewell’s threats fail to rattle Atticus, who rationalizes that taking Ewell’s anger may have spared Mayella further abuse. Jem, however, remains bitter about the jury’s decision and the injustice built into the legal system. Atticus explains that while the legal system may aspire to equality, human prejudice often poisons its judgments. He further asserts that any white man who exploits a Black man is morally bankrupt. Atticus also reveals that at least one juror—a Cunningham—had argued for acquittal, offering a rare sign of progress. Scout suggests inviting Walter Cunningham over, but Aunt Alexandra refuses, exposing the rigid class system embedded even within families that claim to oppose racism. Jem describes Maycomb’s social hierarchy, dividing the town into four social tiers. Jem then reflects on Boo Radley’s isolation, suggesting that Boo may have chosen seclusion to avoid the cruelty and hypocrisy that infect society. Chapter 23 Analysis The trial’s aftermath forces the Finch children into a more nuanced understanding of injustice. Jem’s emerging cynicism is a natural consequence of his disillusionment, while Scout’s instinctual belief in equality reflects her youthful moral clarity. The conversation about social hierarchies demonstrates that racism is only one form of bigotry operating in Maycomb; rigid class distinctions also sustain divisions. Jem’s comment about Boo Radley signals his maturing recognition that sometimes self-imposed exile can be preferable to living within a corrupt society. Chapter 24 Aunt Alexandra hosts a missionary circle meeting, where Maycomb’s white women gather to discuss converting African tribes while expressing patronizing and hypocritical views about their own Black neighbors. They condemn Black citizens for their justified outrage after the trial while pretending moral superiority. Miss Maudie gently but firmly rebukes their hypocrisy. In the midst of the gathering, Atticus arrives with grim news: Tom Robinson has been killed while attempting to escape prison, shot seventeen times. Alexandra is shaken by the news but quickly regains her composure to maintain the appearance of grace for her guests. Scout admires her aunt's dignity and resolves to follow her example. Chapter 24 Analysis This chapter offers one of the novel’s most biting critiques of Southern hypocrisy. The women’s casual racism is all the more chilling because it’s cloaked in the language of piety and charity. Their willingness to discuss the spiritual needs of distant Africans while ignoring the oppression of Black citizens in their own town epitomizes moral blindness. Tom Robinson’s death—brutal and excessive—becomes a horrifying symbol of society’s eagerness to destroy what it has already condemned. Scout’s admiration for Alexandra’s poise reveals her evolving understanding of inner strength—not just physical bravery, but emotional endurance in the face of tragedy.
