Chapter 18

Page 514: Exercise 10

STEP 1
In 1984, conservation biologist Thomas Lovejoy of the World Wildlife Fund proposed an innovative solution to preserve ecosystems while aiding countries facing economic hardship. His idea, known as the "debt-for-nature" swap, was aimed at addressing two problems at once: biodiversity loss and overwhelming national debt in developing nations.

STEP 2
The premise was based on the observation that many poorer nations—particularly those rich in biological diversity—often carry substantial debts owed to wealthier industrialized countries. While loans were originally meant to support economic development, in many cases, these nations struggled to repay the money and had little left over to spend on environmental protection or infrastructure.

STEP 3
Lovejoy's model proposed that environmental organizations or foreign governments could buy or forgive part of a country's debt in return for local conservation commitments. The repurposed funds are redirected to in-country environmental groups, which then use them to finance protected areas, preserve forests, or support biodiversity programs. These exchanges provide dual benefits: relieving financial pressure and enhancing ecological protection.

Result: (b)
