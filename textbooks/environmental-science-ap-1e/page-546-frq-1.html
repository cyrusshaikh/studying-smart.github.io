Chapter 19

Page 546: Exercise 1

STEP 1
The scientific community widely agrees that global warming is occurring and is already impacting natural systems. Numerous environmental indicators—such as rising global temperatures, glacial retreat, and changing precipitation patterns—demonstrate that Earth's climate is shifting in measurable ways.

STEP 2
Data-driven climate models not only explain current trends but also predict future consequences with strong accuracy. These projections suggest that ongoing warming will continue to trigger major environmental disruptions, including the accelerated melting of ice sheets, the thawing of permafrost, and rising ocean levels—many of which are already underway.

STEP 3
The role of greenhouse gases, particularly carbon dioxide, has been rigorously studied and confirmed. Charles David Keeling’s long-term monitoring of atmospheric CO₂ since the 1950s clearly showed both seasonal fluctuations and a consistent year-over-year increase in concentration, a trend that correlates closely with industrial emissions and deforestation.

STEP 4
This rise in atmospheric carbon is not natural—it directly mirrors human activities such as fossil fuel combustion and large-scale land use changes. These activities are now the dominant drivers of greenhouse gas accumulation, surpassing natural sources.

STEP 5
Global warming is not only an environmental issue but also a public health concern. Warming temperatures are expected to expand the range of disease-carrying organisms like mosquitoes, increase the frequency of extreme heat events, and exacerbate respiratory and cardiovascular conditions in vulnerable populations.

STEP 6
Economic impacts are also becoming evident. Warmer winters may temporarily reduce heating costs in some regions, but they also disrupt seasonal industries like skiing and ice fishing. In tropical areas, damage to coral reefs and increased storm intensity threaten both biodiversity and tourism-based economies.
