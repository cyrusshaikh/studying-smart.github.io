Chapter 18

Page 515: Exercise 2a

STEP 1
Conservation planners often face a critical question when establishing protected areas: is it more effective to preserve one large habitat or several smaller ones? This debate is known as the SLOSS dilemma—short for "Single Large Or Several Small."

STEP 2
Each strategy has unique advantages. A large continuous area can support more individuals of a species and offers extensive space for animals that require broad ranges. Larger reserves also reduce "edge effects"—the ecological changes at habitat boundaries that can harm sensitive species.

STEP 3
However, conserving multiple smaller sites can also be beneficial. If these areas are well-distributed and ecologically diverse, they can collectively protect a greater variety of species and habitats. Furthermore, if a disease or natural disaster strikes one reserve, others may remain unaffected, preserving species that might otherwise be lost.

STEP 4
Ultimately, the best choice often depends on context: the landscape, the target species, and human development pressures. In practice, conservation efforts may need to balance both models to maximize ecological resilience and biodiversity protection.
