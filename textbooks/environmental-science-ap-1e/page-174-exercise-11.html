Page 174: Exercise 11

STEP 1
This scenario describes secondary succession, which happens on land that already has existing soil—unlike primary succession, which starts from bare rock. Since the ground is already prepared, the process begins with fast-growing grasses and wildflowers, whose seeds can spread easily by wind. These early colonizers are soon followed by shrubs, which compete for resources like sunlight in these open, abandoned areas.

STEP 2
When rainfall is sufficient, trees begin to grow and replace the shrubs. These early tree species are known as pioneer trees because they establish quickly and require large amounts of sunlight to thrive. As they grow, they develop wide, spreading canopies that compete with one another to absorb as much light as possible.

STEP 3
As these pioneer trees multiply and their canopies thicken, they begin to block sunlight from reaching the forest floor. Over time, this shaded environment favors species that can tolerate low light conditions. Shade-adapted trees like beech, maple, and conifers gradually grow beneath the canopy, eventually replacing or coexisting with the original pioneer species.
