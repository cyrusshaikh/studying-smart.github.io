Chapter 4

Page 116: Exercise 2a

Tropical rainforests are found in regions that receive the most direct sunlight throughout the year. This intense solar energy, combined with warm ocean temperatures and high humidity, creates ideal conditions for dense plant growth.

The Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) shifts across the equator to follow the sun’s most direct rays. As it moves, it influences the wet and dry seasons experienced by biomes near the equator. These shifts in climate patterns are driven by the presence or absence of the ITCZ, which brings moisture and heat when it arrives and drier conditions when it moves away.

At the opposite extreme, the polar regions receive sunlight at a much lower angle. This means solar rays must spread out over a larger surface area, delivering less energy per unit, which results in colder temperatures. Additionally, the bright white surfaces of polar ice caps reflect a large portion of the incoming sunlight back into space, further limiting heat absorption in these areas.
