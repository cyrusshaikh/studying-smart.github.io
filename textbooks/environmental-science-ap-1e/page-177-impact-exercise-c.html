Chapter 6

Page 177: Exercise C

STEP 1
One effective way to help conserve biodiversity is by minimizing the use of paper and paper-based products, especially those made from non-recycled wood sources. Choosing items made entirely from recycled materials reduces the demand for virgin paper products. When more consumers support recycled alternatives, it lowers pressure on forests by decreasing the need to harvest trees for raw materials.

STEP 2
Another helpful measure is to reduce reliance on food products that are heavily cultivated in tropical regions—such as rice, soybeans, sugarcane, beets, and cereals. These crops are often grown in areas cleared of forest, contributing to habitat loss. If consumption of these products declines, so does demand, which in turn can reduce agricultural expansion into forested areas. Preserving forests in these zones helps maintain high levels of biodiversity.

STEP 3
Minimizing environmental pollution is another important action individuals can take. Biodiversity is often threatened by contamination of air, water, and soil, particularly in vulnerable ecosystems. Responsible waste disposal, reduced chemical use, and limiting activities like burning agricultural residue all contribute to a healthier environment and more stable ecosystems.

STEP 4
Avoiding or limiting the use of toxic agricultural chemicals, such as pesticides and herbicides, can also help protect natural ecosystems. These substances can leach into the soil or waterways, damaging plant and animal life. Reducing their use supports healthier habitats and reduces long-term harm to local biodiversity.
