Chapter 10

Page 281: Exercise 2c

STEP 1
To address land-use conflicts between conservation efforts and agricultural interests, the Bureau of Land Management (BLM) could offer farmers access to alternative public grazing lands. These state-owned pastures would match the size and productivity of the farmers’ current holdings, providing sufficient space for livestock grazing and hay production. This solution would reduce pressure on existing private lands and lessen the incentive for farmers to eliminate wildlife perceived as a threat.

STEP 2
By giving farmers new grazing options, the need to aggressively control or eradicate prairie dogs would be minimized. Without the urgency to defend every acre of their own land, farmers might be more open to coexistence strategies that balance agriculture with ecological preservation.

STEP 3
At the same time, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (FWS) could step in to regulate prairie dog populations through science-based population monitoring and selective culling. Rather than allowing unrestricted hunting, FWS would establish guidelines to prevent overpopulation while keeping numbers above extinction risk thresholds.

STEP 4
Such regulations should include designated hunting seasons, restrictions on trapping methods, and enforcement mechanisms. Violations would be met with legal penalties, and regular field inspections would help ensure compliance across affected areas.

STEP 5
To further ease tensions, the government could implement a compensation program for farmers. If prairie dogs cause measurable crop loss or degrade forage availability, affected landowners could be reimbursed either through direct financial support or by receiving equivalent amounts of supplemental feed to offset their losses.
