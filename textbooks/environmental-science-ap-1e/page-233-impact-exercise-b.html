Chapter 8

Page 233: Exercise b

STEP 1
When people consume meat, it drives the demand for raising livestock, which must be fed with plant-based crops such as corn, soybeans, and wheat. To boost the growth and yield of these crops, synthetic fertilizers rich in minerals like calcium, magnesium, and phosphorus are commonly applied.

STEP 2
These minerals are often sourced by mining specific rocks, which are crushed into fine powders and added to fertilizers. Many of these compounds, especially those containing calcium, are water-soluble. As meat consumption rises, so does the need for livestock feed, indirectly increasing the demand for mineral extraction to produce fertilizers.

STEP 3
Excessive mining of these mineral-rich rocks can negatively affect local ecosystems. The removal of these geological layers may reduce areas with fertile soil above them, altering natural landscapes and disrupting local habitats. Furthermore, when rocky hills are leveled to access these materials, it leads to a decline in natural pastureland.

STEP 4
These grasslands play an important role in absorbing atmospheric CO₂. In addition, repeated use of calcium-based fertilizers can increase soil salinity over time, as calcium salts accumulate in the ground. This buildup may alter the soil’s pH balance—one of the key indicators of soil health—and affect its suitability for plant growth.
