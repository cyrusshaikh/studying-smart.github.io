STEP 1
To evaluate a nation's economic performance, economists often rely on national-level indicators focused on production and consumption. One of the most widely used of these is Gross Domestic Product (GDP), which measures the total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country over the span of one year. However, GDP does not account for environmental costs or externalities, which limits its usefulness in sustainability discussions.

STEP 2
Because GDP focuses solely on economic output, it does not reflect whether that growth improves well-being or harms the environment. This is where the Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI) becomes more meaningful. GPI adjusts the traditional GDP by incorporating social and environmental variables, such as income equality, education access, pollution levels, depletion of natural resources, and public health—making it a more holistic tool for evaluating long-term prosperity.
