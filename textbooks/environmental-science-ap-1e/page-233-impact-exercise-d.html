Chapter 8

Page 233: Exercise d

STEP 1
When comparing my diet to that of someone from a developing country, one of the most noticeable differences lies in both the quality and quantity of food. In many developing regions, people often have limited access to diverse and nutrient-rich foods, resulting in lower daily calorie intake and fewer essential nutrients like proteins, vitamins, and minerals.

STEP 2
Their meals tend to rely heavily on staple crops such as rice, corn, or potatoes, with much less consumption of animal products or processed goods. In contrast, my diet includes more refined and animal-based foods—meats, dairy, packaged products—which require multiple stages of industrial processing. The more processed a food is, the more energy and resources it consumes during production, reducing efficiency in terms of food energy transfer across trophic levels.

STEP 3
As a result, the ecological footprint tied to my diet is considerably larger. Producing highly processed or animal-based foods requires more land, water, and fossil fuels than the simpler, plant-heavy diets more common in developing nations. In essence, the more complex and resource-intensive a diet becomes, the greater the environmental impact.
