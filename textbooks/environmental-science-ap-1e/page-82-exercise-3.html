Page 82: Exercise 3

STEP 1
Phosphorus is an essential nutrient needed by both plants and animals for energy transfer, growth, and development. It plays a critical role in agriculture, particularly in crop production. Because it promotes healthy plant growth, phosphorus is often added to soil in the form of fertilizer.

STEP 2
Unlike other nutrient cycles, the phosphorus cycle does not include a gaseous phase. While trace amounts of phosphorus can enter the atmosphere—such as through rainfall—it is not readily soluble in water. As a result, most phosphorus tends to settle at the bottom of oceans and seas in solid form.

STEP 3
This accumulation of phosphorus on the seafloor is vital, supporting the rich biodiversity of marine ecosystems. Marine plants and animals thrive in areas where phosphorus is present. On land, humans mine ancient phosphorus deposits to create fertilizers. The primary sources of phosphorus in terrestrial ecosystems are rocks and plant matter.

STEP 4
Potassium, like phosphorus, also lacks a gaseous stage in its cycle. However, potassium is more water-soluble than phosphorus, making it more vulnerable to being washed away by rainfall. This process, called leaching, can strip the soil of potassium over time—limiting plant growth in areas where the soil becomes deficient.
