Chapter 6

Page 176: Exercise 1b

STEP 1
A density-dependent factor influencing sheep populations is the availability of food. When the population is small, each sheep has access to a greater share of resources. This abundance supports better health, longer lifespans, and increased reproductive success, which causes the population to grow.

STEP 2
As the number of sheep increases, however, competition for limited resources—like food and space—intensifies. With more individuals and the same amount of food, each sheep gets less, which can lower survival and reproduction rates. Eventually, the population may stop growing or even decline, leveling off around the carrying capacity of the environment. The same applies to space: a lower population means more shelter and territory per sheep, while a crowded population leads to limited habitat availability.

STEP 3
In contrast, density-independent factors—like earthquakes, floods, volcanic eruptions, or wildfires—affect the sheep population regardless of how many individuals are present. These environmental events can cause sudden declines in population size, no matter whether the population was small or large to begin with.
