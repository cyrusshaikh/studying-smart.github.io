Chapter 6

Page 176: Exercise 2c

STEP 1
Secondary succession refers to the natural recovery of biodiversity in an area where vegetation was previously disturbed, but where soil remains intact. This process typically begins with simple plant species like grasses and wildflowers, and gradually moves toward the establishment of larger plants and trees.

STEP 2
This type of succession can occur in a variety of disturbed areas—for example, regions affected by wildfires that destroy existing vegetation, or even abandoned human-made structures like settlements, paved lots, or cleared land. Because the soil hasn’t been stripped away, new plant life can quickly begin to grow.

STEP 3
In tropical environments, a fire might wipe out an entire section of forest, clearing both tall trees and understory plants. Soon after, the first wave of vegetation—grasses and small flowering plants—begins to grow. These early colonizers often arrive via wind-dispersed seeds landing on the newly exposed soil.

STEP 4
Thanks to the high rainfall typical of tropical climates, early successional species—including fast-growing pioneer trees like wild cherry—take root quickly. These plants need abundant sunlight and grow rapidly to outcompete smaller vegetation for light. Birds and wind help spread their seeds across the landscape, allowing the forest to gradually rebuild, tree by tree, in a competition for space and sunlight.
