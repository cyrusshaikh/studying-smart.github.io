Chapter 6

Page 174: Exercise 12

STEP 1
The distance between an island and the mainland acts as a major physical barrier that limits the movement of land-based species. The farther away the island is, the harder it becomes for animals and plants to migrate to or from it. This isolation restricts gene flow and species interaction, which can significantly impact the biodiversity found on the island.

STEP 2
Because of this limited contact, species on remote islands tend to evolve in isolation, and their biodiversity grows more slowly. The lack of incoming species, along with restricted genetic exchange, reduces variation and slows down the rate at which new species can establish themselves.

STEP 3
Island size is another critical factor. A smaller island generally offers fewer resources—such as shelter, food, moisture, and usable land. When resources are scarce, they become limiting factors, capping the number of individuals that can survive and reproduce. This also lowers the carrying capacity, making it harder for large or diverse populations to thrive.

STEP 4
In contrast, larger islands with more space and ecological variety—like Madagascar or the Galápagos—can support a wider range of species, even if they are located far from the mainland. These islands often have the environmental conditions needed to support high biodiversity, despite their isolation.

Result: (d)
