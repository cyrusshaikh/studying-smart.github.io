Exercise 2c

Step 1

The environmental impact of dams begins even before they are constructed. Entire areas must be cleared of human populations in preparation for the future reservoir. Once the dam is in place, the natural river channel is often redirected, and large sections of land are flooded to create the reservoir—submerging ecosystems, farmland, and often forests or wetlands. The larger the dam, the greater the land area that ends up underwater.

Step 2

After construction, one of the most significant disruptions is to the river's natural flow. The dam wall acts as a physical barrier, dividing the river into two disconnected parts. This separation prevents aquatic species—especially migratory fish—from traveling upstream to reproduce. These fish species rely on unbroken access to specific spawning grounds they return to each season.

Step 3

The disruption of animal migration has ripple effects throughout the ecosystem. For example, predators like bears depend on seasonal fish runs for nutrition. If fish can no longer reach upstream breeding areas due to the dam, these predators lose access to a vital food source, which can threaten their survival and alter the food chain in the surrounding environment.
