Chapter 7

Page 201: Exercise b

Step 1:
If every person on Earth consumed natural resources at the same rate as individuals with a footprint of 9.0 hectares per capita, then with a global population of 6.8 billion, the total ecological footprint would be:

9.0 ha × 6.8 billion people = 61.2 billion hectares

Step 2:
To determine how many Earth-sized land areas would be needed to support that level of consumption, divide the total global footprint by the planet’s available usable land, which is estimated at 11.4 billion hectares:

61.2 billion ha ÷ 11.4 billion ha = 5.37 Earths

This means over five full Earths would be required to sustain the global population at this rate of resource use.

Step 3:
If everyone in the world had the same ecological footprint as the average person in China (1.8 hectares per person), the total global footprint would be:

1.8 ha × 6.8 billion people = 12.24 billion hectares

Now compare that to the available land:

12.24 billion ha ÷ 11.4 billion ha = 1.07 Earths

So, just slightly more than one Earth would be needed to support global resource use at China’s average footprint level.

Step 4:
If everyone had the same ecological footprint as the average person in India (0.8 hectares per capita), the calculation would be:

0.8 ha × 6.8 billion people = 5.44 billion hectares

5.44 billion ha ÷ 11.4 billion ha = 0.48 Earths

This means less than half of Earth’s usable land would be sufficient if global consumption matched India’s average.

Step 5:
If the average global ecological footprint matched that of Japan (4.1 hectares per person), the total would be:

4.1 ha × 6.8 billion people = 27.88 billion hectares

27.88 billion ha ÷ 11.4 billion ha = 2.45 Earths

So, about two and a half Earths would be required to support humanity at Japan’s rate of resource consumption.
