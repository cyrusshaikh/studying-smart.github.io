Chapter 6

Page 174: Exercise 8

STEP 1
The behavior of these eight different woodpecker species demonstrates a concept known as resource partitioning. Although they all inhabit the same forest ecosystem, each species has evolved in a way that allows them to share space without direct conflict. Instead of competing for the exact same tree or nesting area, they spread out—each adapting to a slightly different ecological niche.

STEP 2
This division of resources reduces competition among the woodpecker species. Rather than fighting over food, shelter, or nesting space, each species utilizes different parts of the habitat, enabling them to coexist peacefully within the same community.

STEP 3
Through microevolution, each woodpecker species has developed preferences for specific types of trees. For instance, smaller woodpeckers may choose decayed or rotting wood, which suits their light body weight and poses little risk of collapse. These subtle specializations have emerged over generations.

STEP 4
Likewise, variations in physical traits such as beak size influence feeding and nesting behavior. Species with larger beaks may prefer trees that offer large seeds or provide dense leaf cover for protection. In contrast, those with smaller beaks may target trees that yield smaller seeds. These adaptations ensure that each species finds its own niche without directly competing with others.
