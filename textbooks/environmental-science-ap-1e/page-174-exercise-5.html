Chapter 6

Page 174: Exercise 5

STEP 1
R-selected species are known for their ability to reproduce at very high rates. These organisms—such as mice, insects like ants and spiders, certain fish, and rats—often produce dozens or even hundreds of offspring in a single reproductive cycle. Instead of having a few well-cared-for young, they produce many and invest little in each one.

STEP 2
Because they grow and reproduce quickly, r-selected species don’t usually hover near the carrying capacity of their environment. Their populations can spike rapidly. Many of these organisms also have short life cycles, meaning it doesn’t take long for them to reach maturity and begin reproducing again, fueling even faster population growth.

STEP 3
However, this strategy has a downside: many of these offspring don’t survive. Without parental care or protection, young individuals are especially vulnerable to predators, starvation, and environmental dangers. As a result, early mortality is common.

STEP 4
Since most r-selected species receive no support from their parents, the young must learn to survive entirely on their own—finding food, avoiding threats, and adapting to their surroundings. While a few succeed, many don’t. Only the most adaptable individuals make it to adulthood, while the rest are lost early in life.

Result: (d)
