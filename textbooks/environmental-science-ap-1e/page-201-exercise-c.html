Chapter 7

Page 201: Exercise c

Step 1:
Based on the data showing national ecological footprints, population sizes, and available land use per country, it can be concluded that if everyone on Earth consumed resources at the same level as the average person in India, slightly more than half of the planet’s usable land would be needed to support global consumption.

Step 2:
In contrast, if the average global resource use matched that of the United States, which has the highest per capita ecological footprint among the countries listed, the world would require approximately 5.3 times the amount of usable land currently available in order to meet demand.

Step 3:
A long-term solution would involve encouraging all countries to live within the limits of their own available resources. In wealthier nations, this would mean reducing individual consumption levels, while in lower-income countries, it would involve increasing resource access and use to improve living conditions and balance disparities.

Step 4:
Achieving a balance between these two extremes—overconsumption in rich nations and underconsumption in poorer ones—would lead to more effective and equitable land resource management. Overuse of land, as seen in high-consumption countries like the U.S., results in environmental strain, while low consumption in high-population countries like India can lead to poverty and resource scarcity.

Step 5:
To improve sustainability in regions with very low per capita resource use, population growth may need to be addressed. Reducing population pressure would help raise the average ecological footprint, ensuring people have better access to resources. This would allow all nations to operate within their own ecological limits and promote a more balanced, sustainable global system.
