Chapter 8

Page 232: Exercise 1b

STEP 1
Physical weathering is the process by which solid rocks break down into smaller fragments without changing their chemical composition. This type of weathering is most common in areas with extreme climates, such as high mountain regions, polar zones, and deserts, where the environment causes rocks to fracture and crumble over time.

STEP 2
One of the main drivers of physical weathering is temperature fluctuation. In desert environments, for example, rocks are exposed to intense heat during the day and rapid cooling at night. These sharp shifts cause the outer layers of rock to expand and contract, eventually leading to cracking and fragmentation. Similarly, in cold regions, frost action is a powerful force: water seeps into tiny cracks in the rock, freezes, expands, and gradually pries the rock apart.

STEP 3
Another contributor to mechanical breakdown is biological activity, particularly the growth of plant roots. When seeds take root in the crevices of rocks, the roots grow and thicken over time. As they expand, they exert pressure on the surrounding rock, causing it to split and break apart. This is a form of organogenic weathering, where living organisms drive the mechanical breakdown of the Earth's surface.
