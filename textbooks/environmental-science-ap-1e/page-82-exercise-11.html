Chapter 3

Page 82: Exercise 11

STEP 1
The amount of energy available at the tertiary consumer level is 10 kilocalories (kCal).

STEP 2
Trophic levels beneath the tertiary level include the secondary and primary consumers. Energy moves through these levels in a food chain, and ecological efficiency refers to how effectively energy is passed from one level to the next—starting from producers and moving upward.

STEP 3
This transfer of energy is usually represented as a percentage. Most of the energy in an ecosystem originates from producers, such as plants, and only a small portion of that energy is transferred as it moves up each trophic level. The amount of transferred energy decreases substantially at higher levels.

STEP 4
As we reach the top of the food chain, very little energy remains. Top-level consumers—like lions, bears, or humans—occupy the highest trophic levels and typically have no predators. In this scenario, we are calculating how much of the original energy remains by the time it reaches the tertiary level, which is the third step in the energy pyramid.

STEP 5
The process begins with an original energy input of 10,000 kCal at the producer level. Since only 10% of energy is passed from one level to the next, we calculate the energy available at the primary consumer level:
10,000 × 0.10 = 1,000 kCal

STEP 6
Then, we calculate 10% of the primary consumer level energy to find what reaches the secondary consumers:
1,000 × 0.10 = 100 kCal

STEP 7
Finally, we calculate 10% of the secondary consumer energy to find the amount available at the tertiary level:
100 × 0.10 = 10 kCal

Result: (b)
