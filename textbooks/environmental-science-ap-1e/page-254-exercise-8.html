Chapter 9

Page 254: Exercise 8

STEP 1
Irrigation plays a crucial role in supporting global agriculture but also accounts for a major share of freshwater usage. There are four primary irrigation methods: furrow irrigation, flood irrigation, spray irrigation, and drip irrigation—each with its own efficiency and environmental impact.

STEP 2
Furrow irrigation is one of the oldest and least efficient techniques. Farmers dig shallow trenches alongside crop rows and channel water through them. While inexpensive and simple, this method wastes a significant amount of water through evaporation and runoff, meaning only a portion of it reaches the plant roots.

STEP 3
Flood irrigation involves covering entire crop fields with water. Though relatively easy and low-cost, this method can lead to overwatering, which may harm certain crops. It also promotes the growth of unwanted vegetation like weeds, since water is distributed uniformly across all soil areas, not just near the crop roots.

STEP 4
Spray irrigation uses pressurized systems—such as sprinklers or pumps—to distribute water over crops. This method delivers water more directly to the plants and is far more efficient than furrow or flood irrigation, with up to 90% of the water reaching the crops. However, it requires more infrastructure and energy input.

STEP 5
Drip irrigation is considered the most efficient system, with more than 95% water use efficiency. It involves narrow tubing that releases water slowly and precisely at the base of each plant, either above or just below the soil. This not only minimizes water waste through evaporation and runoff but also limits the growth of weeds by targeting only the crops.

Result: (c)
