Chapter 18

Page 514: Exercise 9

STEP 1
To help manage protected environments such as national parks in a balanced and sustainable way, the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) introduced the concept of biosphere reserves. This approach allows areas of natural significance to be both preserved and responsibly used, minimizing harm while maximizing their benefits for research, conservation, and community engagement.

STEP 2
Biosphere reserves are specially designated areas that include multiple zones, each with differing levels of allowed human activity. These zones work together to protect ecosystems while supporting sustainable interaction between people and nature.

STEP 3
Each biosphere reserve generally contains three key zones:

Core area: This inner region is strictly protected, with little to no human interference. It serves as a sanctuary for wildlife and is critical for conserving biodiversity.

Buffer zone: Surrounding the core, this area supports limited human activities like research, education, and eco-tourism—all of which are carefully managed to avoid harming the core.

Transition zone: The outermost region, where sustainable practices such as farming, forestry, and community development can take place. People live and work here in ways that align with environmental goals.

STEP 4
Although biosphere reserves vary widely in shape and size depending on the location, their zoning structure is consistent: a protected core, surrounded by zones that allow for increasing levels of human involvement. Globally, there are currently 564 designated biosphere reserves across 109 countries, including 47 in the United States.

Result: Correct Answer: (a) – This option correctly represents the purpose and structure of biosphere reserves.
