Chapter 6

Page 176: Exercise 1a

STEP 1
A metapopulation refers to a group of animal populations that are separated into distinct habitats but still connected in some way—usually through occasional movement or migration. These animals thrive in favorable environments that match their biological needs, but cannot easily survive in surrounding regions with harsh or unsuitable conditions. For example, certain areas may be too dry, hot, or barren for species like sheep to inhabit successfully.

STEP 2
In the case of sheep, they tend to inhabit mountainous regions that offer ideal conditions for their survival, growth, and reproduction. These mountains, which are often scattered across desert landscapes, act like "habitat islands". Even though these patches are separated by less hospitable terrain, sheep can move between them using corridors—narrow strips of land that connect different populations. This movement allows interaction between separate sheep groups, helping to maintain genetic diversity and ecological balance across the broader metapopulation.
