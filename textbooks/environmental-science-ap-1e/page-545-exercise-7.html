Chapter 19

Page 545: Exercise 7

STEP 1
To answer this question, we need to determine which types of evidence provide reliable data on long-term changes in global temperature and atmospheric CO₂ levels.

STEP 2
One powerful source is ice core samples extracted from glaciers and polar ice sheets. As snow compresses into ice, it traps tiny pockets of ancient air. These bubbles serve as time capsules, preserving the composition of the atmosphere—including CO₂ and methane levels—from thousands of years ago.

STEP 3
In modern times, temperature monitoring stations located worldwide—often referred to as global thermometer networks—record surface temperature changes across large regions. These consistent measurements are key to tracking modern climate trends.

STEP 4
In addition, carbon dioxide sensors placed in strategic locations (like Mauna Loa in Hawaii) detect and record real-time changes in atmospheric CO₂ concentration. This data allows scientists to assess current emission trends and correlate them with temperature shifts.

Result: (e) — All of these sources—ice cores, temperature stations, and CO₂ sensors—are valid tools for studying global climate and emissions patterns.
