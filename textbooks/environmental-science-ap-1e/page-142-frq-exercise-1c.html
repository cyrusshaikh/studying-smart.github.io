Chapter 5

Page 142: Free-Response Exercise 1c

STEP 1
Microevolution refers to small-scale changes that occur within a single species, whether plants or animals. These gradual changes happen over generations through genetic inheritance but do not result in the formation of new species. Essentially, microevolution involves variations within the species’ gene pool that influence individual traits.

STEP 2
In contrast, macroevolution involves larger genetic changes at the species level, where evolutionary processes such as natural selection or random mutations lead to the emergence of entirely new species. A defining feature of macroevolution is that newly formed species are reproductively isolated—they cannot interbreed with their ancestral species.

STEP 3
This reproductive isolation often arises when populations of the same species become geographically separated—such as living in two distinct habitats—leading each group to evolve differently in response to their unique environments. Over time, these differences accumulate, producing distinct phenotypes adapted to their surroundings.

STEP 4
The macroevolutionary process unfolds gradually, requiring long periods during which genetic changes accumulate without interbreeding between populations. Physical barriers, like geographic separations, are critical because they prevent gene flow, allowing new species to form.

STEP 5
Consider a forest divided by a road that acts as a physical barrier restricting animal movement. The same species may inhabit both parts but with limited interaction due to the obstacle, effectively isolating populations in forest A and forest B.

STEP 6
For species that move slowly—like turtles—this barrier is particularly significant. Their limited mobility reduces the chances of crossing between forests, leading to minimal contact between the two populations.

STEP 7
Over many generations, the populations in forest A and forest B will accumulate genetic differences as they adapt independently to their environments. The lack of gene flow and breeding between these groups allows distinct genetic traits to develop.

STEP 8
Eventually, the turtle populations in the two forests will diverge enough to become separate species, incapable of interbreeding. This slow but continuous evolutionary process results in unique genetic characteristics in each population, highlighting the formation of new species through geographic isolation.
