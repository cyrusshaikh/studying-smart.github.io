Chapter 6

Page 199: Exercise 2

STEP 1
Two of the most important indicators of a country’s health care quality are life expectancy and the infant mortality rate. These measures reflect how well a society supports the health and longevity of its population.

STEP 2
Life expectancy refers to the average number of years a person is expected to live, based on the conditions in their environment. In wealthier, more developed countries, access to modern health services, clean water, adequate food, and medicine leads to longer life spans. In contrast, in less developed nations where these essentials are limited, life expectancy is typically shorter. It is also common for women to live longer than men on average, regardless of country.

STEP 3
The infant mortality rate measures the number of infants (under 1 year old) who die per 1,000 live births. A related figure, the child mortality rate, tracks deaths before age five. Both are deeply influenced by the availability of medical care, clean environments, and access to proper nutrition.

STEP 4
In developed nations, strong health care systems, low pollution levels, and sufficient resources result in low infant mortality rates—meaning most babies survive beyond infancy. There are fewer deaths compared to births due to strong public health infrastructure.

STEP 5
In contrast, underdeveloped regions often struggle with limited health care access, polluted air and water, and resource scarcity. This leads to higher infant mortality rates, sometimes even exceeding the birth rate. Global comparisons show wide gaps—for instance, the United States has a significantly lower infant mortality rate than countries like India or those in Africa, largely due to differences in infrastructure and economic development.
