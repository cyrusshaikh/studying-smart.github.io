Page 115: Exercise 6

STEP 1
Scientists identify biomes by grouping together plant species that require similar climatic conditions, such as temperature and rainfall, for their growth. A biome is a large ecological community made up of species with shared traits that have evolved in response to Earth's varying climate zones.

STEP 2
For instance, dry regions around the world will generally support one type of biome, while humid areas support entirely different ones. This is because plants adapted to arid environments have very different characteristics from those that thrive in moist, water-rich regions.

STEP 3
By analyzing the patterns of plant and animal life, scientists are able to identify and classify biomes—ecosystems where species are adapted to similar climatic factors. These biomes are found on every continent.

STEP 4
Plants within a particular biome tend to share similar sizes, shapes, colors, and other features. These characteristics are closely tied to the local temperature, humidity, and precipitation levels. For example, some biomes include plant species adapted to very cold climates, while others consist of species that survive best in hotter environments.

STEP 5
The same principle applies to water availability. Certain plants, like desert cacti, can survive with very little rainfall, while others, such as those in tropical rainforests, require heavy, year-round precipitation.

STEP 6
Using these traits, scientists can more accurately determine which species belong to which biome. There are three primary types of terrestrial biomes:

  • Tundra and boreal forests
  • Temperate forests
  • Tropical forests

Each of these can be further divided into more specific subcategories.

Result: (d)
