Page 404: Exercise 4

STEP 1
This question asks where bacterial decomposition of organic matter takes place in septic systems and municipal sewage treatment plants.

STEP 2
A septic system is a decentralized method of wastewater treatment commonly used in sparsely populated or rural areas. It generally includes two main components: a septic tank and a leach field.

STEP 3
The septic tank collects all wastewater from the household. Inside the tank, heavier solids settle to form sludge, while lighter materials and relatively clear water—known as septage—remain above. This septage still contains bacteria and dissolved organic matter.

STEP 4
The leach field consists of underground pipes buried under a lawn or field. The septage is distributed through these pipes and filters into the surrounding soil. Microorganisms in the soil break down the organic compounds, converting them into simpler nutrients and carbon dioxide.

STEP 5
In sewage treatment plants, which serve densely populated areas, a centralized network of pipes collects and directs wastewater to a treatment facility.

STEP 6
These facilities typically process wastewater in two key phases:

Primary treatment physically removes solids from the water.

Secondary treatment biologically treats the water by using bacteria to break down organic matter into inorganic nutrients and carbon dioxide.

STEP 7
Therefore, the breakdown of organic matter by bacteria happens specifically during the secondary treatment stage in sewage treatment plants.

Result: (d)
