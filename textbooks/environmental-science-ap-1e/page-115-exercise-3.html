Page 115: Exercise 3

Solution 1

STEP 1
Hadley cells are convection systems that dominate low-latitude regions on both sides of the equator, extending roughly from 30° North to 30° South.

STEP 2
These currents move heat energy away from the equator. As warm air rises at the equator and moves toward 30°N and 30°S, it cools, causing a drop in atmospheric pressure. The cooling leads to condensation of water vapor, which results in cloud formation and precipitation. This process also releases latent heat, causing the air to expand and continue rising until it reaches the upper boundary of the troposphere.

STEP 3
Air at the top of the troposphere is cooler due to the higher altitude. The rising warm air pushes this cooler, drier air away from the equator, spreading it toward both hemispheres.

STEP 4
Eventually, this elevated air sinks back down around 30° latitude, where it warms again due to increased atmospheric pressure as it returns to lower altitudes.

Result: (e)

Solution 2

STEP 1
In a Hadley cell, warm air rises near the equator, then moves poleward in the upper atmosphere. This movement forms part of a global convection cycle.

Result: (e)

Solution 3

STEP 1
Atmospheric convection currents are driven by the uneven heating of Earth’s surface. These currents create predictable global air circulation patterns.

Hadley Cell: This cell involves air rising at the equator and cycling between 30°N and 30°S.

STEP 2
Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ): This is the region near the equator where the sun’s rays are most direct, and where air from both Hadley cells converges and rises.

Polar Cell: This circulation cell forms when air rises at 60°N and 60°S and sinks at the poles (90°N/S).

STEP 3
A key incorrect statement about Hadley cells is:

“Along Earth’s surface, the air of a Hadley cell moves away from the equator.”

In reality, surface air in a Hadley cell moves toward the equator, not away from it.

Result: (e)

Solution 4

The statement:

“Along Earth's surface, the air of a Hadley cell moves away from the equator.”

is false, because in a Hadley cell, surface winds flow toward the equator, not away.

Result: (e)
