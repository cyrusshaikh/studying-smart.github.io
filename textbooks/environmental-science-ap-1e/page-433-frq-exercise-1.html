Chapter 15

Page 433: Exercise 1

STEP 1
To answer this question, we must evaluate air quality data recorded in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania in 2008.

STEP 2
a) According to the National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS), the acceptable limit for ozone is 75 parts per billion (ppb) over any 8-hour average period. The data from Pittsburgh in 2008 shows that the highest recorded monthly average ozone concentration was 32 ppb in June, which means the city met the standard for that year.

STEP 3
b) Air pollutants are substances in the atmosphere that can harm living beings and ecosystems. They fall into two main categories:

Primary pollutants, which are released directly from a source, such as emissions from engines or evaporation of chemicals.

Secondary pollutants, which are formed when primary pollutants undergo chemical transformations in the atmosphere, often triggered by heat or sunlight.

STEP 4
One common example of a secondary pollutant is ozone (O₃). It forms when nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) react in the presence of sunlight through a process called photochemical smog formation.

STEP 5
Ozone exists in both the troposphere (lower atmosphere) and stratosphere (upper atmosphere), but its effects vary depending on altitude.

In the stratosphere, ozone occurs naturally and helps block harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun.

In the troposphere, however, ozone is man-made and forms through sunlight-driven reactions with pollutants. This ground-level ozone is harmful to humans, especially to the lungs and respiratory system.

STEP 6
c) The data shows a positive correlation between average monthly ozone concentrations and levels of solar radiation. As more sunlight reaches the Earth's surface, more photochemical reactions occur, leading to an increase in ozone production. A similar upward trend is seen between solar radiation and maximum daily ozone values each month.

STEP 7
d) To summarize: the natural ozone layer in the stratosphere protects life by filtering UV rays, while human-caused ozone in the troposphere contributes to air pollution and poses health risks to humans and animals, especially by affecting the lungs and overall respiratory health.
