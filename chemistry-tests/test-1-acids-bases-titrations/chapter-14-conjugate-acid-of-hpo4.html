Which of the following is the conjugate acid of HPO₄²⁻?

Question:
Which of the following is the conjugate acid of HPO₄²⁻?

  • a) H₂PO₄⁻
  • b) PO₄³⁻
  • c) H₃PO₄
  • d) H₃O⁺

Scan of Original Question

Handwritten Solution

Full Explanation

When identifying a conjugate acid, you're looking for the species formed when the original base gains a proton (H⁺).

HPO₄²⁻ + H⁺ → H₂PO₄⁻

a) H₂PO₄⁻ — Correct
This is the conjugate acid of HPO₄²⁻. H₂PO₄⁻ has one more hydrogen than HPO₄²⁻ and one fewer negative charge (going from -2 to -1). That’s exactly what happens when a base accepts an H⁺ proton.

This follows the Bronsted-Lowry definition: acids donate H⁺, and bases accept H⁺. Since HPO₄²⁻ accepts a proton, H₂PO₄⁻ is its conjugate acid.

b) PO₄³⁻ — Incorrect
This is actually the conjugate base of HPO₄²⁻. It has one fewer hydrogen and one more negative charge, which means it results from losing a proton, not gaining one.

c) H₃PO₄ — Incorrect
H₃PO₄ has two more protons than HPO₄²⁻. It's a fully protonated acid, not the direct conjugate. H₂PO₄⁻ is the appropriate one-proton-up species.

d) H₃O⁺ — Incorrect
H₃O⁺ is a proton donor, not a phosphate species. While it can donate a proton to HPO₄²⁻, it is not the conjugate acid of HPO₄²⁻ itself.

✅ Final Answer: a) H₂PO₄⁻ — It is exactly one proton more than HPO₄²⁻, making it the direct conjugate acid.
