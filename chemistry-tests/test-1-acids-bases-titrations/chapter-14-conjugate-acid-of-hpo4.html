Which of the following is the conjugate acid of HPO₄²⁻?

a) H₂PO₄⁻

b) PO₄³⁻

c) H₃PO₄

d) H₃O⁺

✅ a) H₂PO₄⁻ — Correct

This is the conjugate acid of HPO₄²⁻. It has one more hydrogen and one fewer negative charge (from -2 to -1), which is exactly what happens when a species gains an H⁺ proton.

This follows the Bronsted-Lowry rule: acids donate H⁺, and bases accept it. Since HPO₄²⁻ accepts an H⁺, H₂PO₄⁻ is its conjugate acid.

❌ b) PO₄³⁻ — Incorrect

This is the conjugate base of HPO₄²⁻. It has lost a proton, not gained one. It's going in the wrong direction.

❌ c) H₃PO₄ — Incorrect

This has two more protons than HPO₄²⁻. It's not the immediate conjugate acid — it's further up the chain (the fully protonated phosphoric acid).

❌ d) H₃O⁺ — Incorrect

While H₃O⁺ can donate a proton to HPO₄²⁻, it’s not the conjugate acid of HPO₄²⁻. It's a separate species — a general acid, not a phosphate-based conjugate.

✅ Final Answer: a) H₂PO₄⁻
